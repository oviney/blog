# Editorial Review: Shift-Right: The Trend Nobody Budgeted For

The article does an impressive job of presenting the shift-right testing trend and backing it up with sourced data. However, there are a few areas where improvements can be made:

1. **Cliché**
   - Problematic text: "in an era dominated by rapid technological integration."
   - Why it's a problem: The phrase "in an era" is overused and adds little value to the sentence.
   - Suggested fix: Remove the cliché and rewrite for clarity. For example, "as technology continues to integrate rapidly into all spheres."

2. **Logic Gaps**
   - Problematic text: "Despite the spending, some sectors report stagnation in incident resolution times, suggesting that the solution may lie beyond technology enhancement and point towards a necessary upskilling of staff to fully harness these tools."
   - Why it's a problem: The relationship between spending, stagnation in incident resolution times, and the need for upskilling isn't well clarified.
   - Suggested fix: Provide more examples or case studies to illustrate how lack of skills directly impacts incident resolution times despite increased spending.

3. **Missing Contrarian**
   - Problematic text: The entire article presents shift-right and observability in overwhelmingly positive light with no mention of potential downsides or challenges.
   - Why it's a problem: Good analysis should include potential criticism or challenges associated with adopting such practices.
   - Suggested fix: Include a paragraph discussing possible drawbacks or concerns, such as the potential for increased complexity in production systems or initial resistance from teams used to traditional QA methods.

4. **Voice Break**
   - Problematic text: "However, the effectiveness of AI integration depends largely on the skill level and preparedness of the teams using these advanced systems."
   - Why it's a problem: The sentence structure is somewhat informal and lacks the concise, analytical tone typical of The Economist.
   - Suggested fix: Revise to a more succinct statement, e.g., "The success of AI integration is contingent upon the competence and readiness of the users engineering these systems."

Overall, while the article is informative and well-sourced, these refinements could enhance clarity, balance, and adherence to The Economist's style.
