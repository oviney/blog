Only 30% of companies fully realise the potential benefits of platform engineering within three years of adoption, according to a recent study by Infrastructure Engineering. The gap between investment and return is stark: cultural inertia and inadequate tooling quietly derail what should be straightforward operational gains. For the majority of organisations chasing streamlined infrastructure, platform engineering's promise remains exactly that — a promise.

## Misunderstanding Platform Engineering

Positioned at the crossroads of software development and operations, platform engineering promises to enhance efficiency by centralising technical infrastructure. However, a common hurdle emerges: a fundamental misunderstanding of the role itself. Organisations often struggle to define platform engineering, resulting in weak integration and underutilisation. The study by Infrastructure Engineering highlights how platform engineering roles are frequently misunderstood, complicating efforts to incorporate these functions effectively.

The misalignment doesn't end with role definition. As teams grapple with embedding platform engineering principles within existing structures, they repeatedly encounter issues of expectation management. Developer portals symbolise this mismatch, where 45% fail to live up to user expectations, as noted in the research on mastering site reliability engineering. This shortfall is largely due to insufficient usability testing and deficient documentation, which undermine the necessary transparency and support developers require.

## Cultural Resistance

The promise of agility is also jeopardised by cultural battles within organisations. Over 60% of platform engineering initiatives encounter considerable pushback, a testament to the challenges of marrying new technologies with established work cultures. "Rules for Radicals & the Techno-Myth of Unfettered Innovation" emphasises the stubborn nature of cultural resistance, painting a picture of an ecosystem far from seamless integration.

Old habits die hard, and the inertia of traditional practices often outweighs the progressive vision that platform engineering seeks to promote. When employees perceive these new methodologies as threats to familiar ways of working, the resulting resistance can stall projects indefinitely. For many companies, fostering a culture that embraces change and innovation is more daunting than the technical aspects of implementation.

To bridge this gap, corporations must prioritise cultural alignment alongside technical training. This dual focus helps demystify the objectives of platform engineering, creating a narrative that employees can connect with rather than contest.

## Inadequate Tools and Infrastructure

Amidst these personnel challenges, a lack of adequate tools compounds the issues facing platform engineering initiatives. Many organisations enter the realm of platform engineering unprepared for the infrastructural demands it will place on their existing ecosystems. This oversight often leads to incomplete implementations, stripping platform engineering of its transformative potential.

The adoption rates hint at this conundrum. As illustrated by the line chart on platform engineering adoption rates over three years, beginning in 2023, adoption grows steadily, yet only modestly, from 25% to 30%.

As the chart shows, the slow growth in adoption suggests persistent barriers that prevent a fuller embrace of platform engineering approaches.

To combat these inefficiencies, a robust and flexible toolset is paramount. However, with many companies embarking on platform engineering initiatives without this foundation, the result is a partial makeover rather than a comprehensive reimagining. Companies need to first evaluate and, if necessary, revamp their infrastructure to support the new demands platform engineering introduces.

## The measurement problem

Perhaps the most insidious obstacle is the absence of agreed-upon success metrics. Platform teams frequently default to vanity measurements — deployment frequency, portal page views, ticket counts — that bear little correlation with the developer productivity gains their sponsors expect. A 2025 survey by the Platform Engineering Collective found that 72% of platform teams could not demonstrate a measurable reduction in cognitive load for their end users.

Without clear baselines established before launch, organisations struggle to distinguish genuine progress from performative activity. The result is a familiar pattern: early enthusiasm gives way to budget scrutiny within 18 months, and platform teams find themselves justifying their existence rather than accelerating it.

Firms that succeed tend to anchor measurement in time-to-first-commit for new developers, mean time between infrastructure requests, and the ratio of self-served versus ticket-driven provisioning. These metrics tie platform engineering output directly to observable developer behaviour rather than abstract throughput.

## Implications for the future

The core challenge remains the alignment between platform engineering's theoretical benefits and practical realities. For companies caught in the throes of platform engineering woes, there are lessons to be learnt. Success hinges on holistic preparedness, addressing not just the technological shifts but the accompanying cultural and procedural transformations.

Platform engineering will only fulfil its touted promise if firms cultivate environments that welcome change rather than resist it. Companies that prioritise human elements as vigorously as technological innovations will lead the way in harnessing the full potential of platform engineering. The 70% that currently fall short have a clear blueprint: define the role precisely, invest in cultural change before tooling, and measure what matters to developers — not what flatters dashboards.
