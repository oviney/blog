Global observability spend is expected to grow from $3.5 billion in 2023 to $6.9 billion by 2026, according to Gartner. This exponential increase underscores a shift in priority towards monitoring and managing live applications, a practice increasingly critical for supporting AI-enhanced and machine learning systems. Observability is gaining traction, not merely as a trend, but as a necessity for organisations striving for agility in an era dominated by rapid technological integration.

## The Shift-Right Approach

Shift-right testing, an approach that involves testing in the actual production environment, is remodelling the landscape of quality assurance. Traditional testing predominantly focused on early stages, but as systems grow more complex, there is a clear movement towards testing at the point of real-world application. Production testing now accounts for approximately 40% of typical IT budgets, indicating a substantial reallocation of resources towards ensuring system reliability post-deployment (Forrester, 2024).

This strategy allows developers to diagnose issues in real time. For example, debugging times have reported a 30% reduction through the adoption of shift-right practices (Aberdeen Group, 2025). Such efficiencies are not just cost-saving but also enhance product reliability and user satisfaction, offering a tangible ROI that justifies the upfront investment and operational changes required.

## The Role of Observability

As the industry pivots to a shift-right methodology, observability becomes indispensable. Observability tools provide insights by continuously monitoring systems, thereby enabling teams to detect, diagnose, and remedy issues swiftly. The surging investment—nearly double by 2026 as anticipated—is a response to the need for real-time data about application health and user interactions.

![Growth of Observability Spend](/assets/charts/shift-right-the-trend-nobody-budgeted-for.png)

As the chart shows, the steady increase in observability spending reflects its growing importance. Investments in these tools ensure that businesses can adapt swiftly to changes and maintain performance standards amid complex, adaptive systems. Despite the spending, some sectors report stagnation in incident resolution times, suggesting that the solution may lie beyond technology enhancement and point towards a necessary upskilling of staff to fully harness these tools.

## AI Integration in Testing

Integrating artificial intelligence within production testing yields promising results, notably a 25% reduction in overall costs (McKinsey & Company, 2025). AI aids in automating mundane tasks, analysing vast datasets for anomalies, and predicting potential failures before they occur. By doing so, it extends the reach of observability systems, offering predictive insights that allow developers to address potential issues proactively instead of reactively.

However, the effectiveness of AI integration depends largely on the skill level and preparedness of the teams using these advanced systems. The challenges posed by AI are not merely technological but also organisational, requiring shifts in company culture to prioritise continuous learning and adaptation.

## Addressing the Skills Gap

The growing observability spend juxtaposed with unchanged incident resolution times highlights a critical disconnect: a skills gap. This gap is becoming a bottleneck, preventing organisations from fully realising the benefits of their investments in advanced testing and monitoring systems. As systems and processes become more sophisticated, so too must the teams tasked with managing them.

Organisations must prioritise upskilling their workforce, focusing on both technical proficiency and strategic thinking. Training programmes tailored to cutting-edge tools and methodologies can bridge this gap, aligning tech capabilities with operational goals. Without this focus, the disparity between tool capability and user efficiency will continue to widen.

## Conclusion: Investing in Skills for Success

Shift-right testing and observability are poised to reshape quality assurance, but the real value of these technologies hinges not just on their implementation, but on their effective use. The onus is now on organisations to match their investment in technology with equivalent commitments to upskilling their personnel. If this balance is achieved, the promise of enhanced efficiency, improved reliability, and greater cost savings can indeed materialise, propelling businesses into a new era of quality engineering excellence.
